Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about electric vehicles

Most modern electric vehicles offer a range between 200-350 miles on a single charge. Premium models like Tesla can achieve over 400 miles, while more affordable options typically provide 150-250 miles. Range varies based on driving conditions, speed, weather, and vehicle efficiency.

Charging time depends on the charger type and battery capacity. Level 1 (home outlet) takes 8-20 hours for a full charge. Level 2 (home/public charger) takes 4-8 hours. DC fast chargers can charge from 10% to 80% in 15-45 minutes, depending on the vehicle's charging capability.

While EVs typically have a higher upfront cost, they often cost less to own over time due to lower fuel and maintenance costs. Federal and state tax credits can reduce the purchase price significantly. Additionally, electricity costs are generally lower than gasoline, and EVs require less maintenance since they have fewer moving parts.

Most EVs provide multiple warnings as the battery level drops, giving you plenty of time to find a charging station. If you do run out, roadside assistance services can tow your vehicle to the nearest charging station. Many EVs also have apps that help you locate nearby charging stations along your route.

Modern EV batteries are designed to last 15-20 years or 100,000-200,000 miles. Most manufacturers offer 8-year/100,000-mile warranties on battery packs. Battery degradation is gradual, typically losing about 2-3% capacity per year. Proper charging habits (avoiding frequent full charges and deep discharges) can help extend battery life.

Yes! You can charge an EV at home using a standard 120V outlet (Level 1), though it's slow. Most EV owners install a Level 2 charger (240V), which requires professional installation but charges 4-5 times faster. Many utility companies offer rebates for home charger installation, and there are federal tax credits available as well.
